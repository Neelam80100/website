THE KITCHEN

Wary of opting for trends they'd later regret, the couple's decisions skewed highly practical. They opted for open shelves on one side of the kitchen to house their most frequently used supplies, but white upper cabinets on the other side. Quartz countertops with a little visible grain made more sense than high-maintenance marble, and subway tile was a no-brainer-except they opted for squares rather than rectangles just to keep it from feeling too Pinteresty. A local cabinetmaker matched a soft green Farrow & Ball shade for the lower cabinets, "to bring in a little color but not make it too overwhelming."

Conveniently, Amber's dad is "very handy," which seems to be putting it lightly. He not only custom-built a banquette for added storage in the breakfast area, but also carefully leveled and trimmed the baseboards to match the lines of the rolling floor so there wouldn't be any gaps. He added long drawers, one to pull out from either end of the bench, and solved an outlet crisis by wiring one to the outside of the banquette so that it wasn't lost with the addition. The couple planned for added storage like this in both the kitchen and bath even though they don't really need it yet. "We kept asking ourselves how we could grow into the space if we're still living in it in 15 years, if we have kids," Adam explains, "because ultimately you're not going to get less things over time."

THE BATH

To maximize space (and meet code) in the closet-size bath, they laid out various configurations on drawing paper and were secretly delighted to realize that a floating vanity was their best bet. "It was kind of nice because it gave us an excuse to get one," Amber says, "even though it doesn't seem practical for storage reasons. It's a lot more cost-effective as well." They solved the storage quandary by mounting shelves-shallow ones by the sink, deeper ones above the toilet-and painting a stool from the thrift store to act as the towel rack. But before all that came the tile (another masterpiece from Amber's dad ).

THE DINING

Balancing out the open-concept space did wonders. Flanigan switched out the stout original upper cabinets with double-stacked versions that reach the ceiling and can stand up to the towering fireplace across the way.
